HUMAN-COMPUTER INTERACTION

What is HCI?

Human-computer interaction (HCI) is defined as a multidisciplinary field of study that focuses on optimizing how humans (the users) and computers interact with each other by designing interactive computer interfaces that satisfy their needs.

Initially the main focus was on the technical and computers aspects, now HCI has expanded to cover almost all forms of information technology design. Therefore, this establishes HCI as a technology that brings different fields and areas together such as computer engineering, cognitive psychology, computer science, behavioural sciences, ergonomics and design principles.

HISTORY OF HCI

When we talk about Human-computer interaction, it is important to first define man-machine interaction illustrated during the 70’. Because the focus was the design applied to everyday life objects such as furniture and their usability. Until the late 1970s, the only humans who interacted with computers were information technology professionals and dedicated hobbyists. However, the technology evolved rapidly, shifting and moving more the focus on machine and computer (textual interface).

Therefore, HCI surfaced in the 1980s with the advent of personal computing. And everyone started talking about “Human-computer” interaction, attracting professionals from many other disciplines and incorporating diverse concepts and approaches. Computers were available and widely spread in household, and this created a need to define a specific field which related software and hardware design. Machines such as the Apple Macintosh, IBM PC 5150 and Commodore 64 started turning up in homes and offices in society-changing numbers.
For the first time, sophisticated electronic systems were available to general consumers for use. Consequently, as computers were no longer room-sized, expensive tools exclusively built for experts in specialized environments, the need to create human-computer interaction that was also easy and efficient for less experienced users.

By the early 1990s, the focus of HCI shifted away from computers and graphical interfaces to usability evaluation, interaction design and user experience. In fact, the technical focus of HCI was and is still the concept of usability. This concept was originally articulated somewhat naively in the slogan "easy to learn, easy to use". Usability is an emergent quality that reflects the grasp and the reach of HCI, contemporary users want more from a system than merely “ease of use”. HCI includes social and organizational computing, accessibility for the elderly, the cognitively and physically impaired, and for all people, and for the widest possible spectrum of human experiences and activities.

The remarkable growth of HCI over the past 30 years has transformed this nascent interdisciplinary field into a worldwide phenomenon, with a huge impact on daily life activities. Today, HCI is a vast and multifaceted community, bound by the evolving concept of usability, and the integrating commitment to value human activity and experience as the primary driver in technology.

“…it no longer makes sense to regard HCI as a specialty of computer science; HCI has grown to be broader, larger and much more diverse than computer science itself. HCI expanded from its initial focus on individual and generic user behaviour to include social and organizational computing, accessibility for the elderly, the cognitively and physically impaired, and for all people, and for the widest possible spectrum of human experiences and activities. It expanded from desktop office applications to include games, learning and education, commerce, health and medical applications, emergency planning and response, and systems to support collaboration and community. It expanded from early graphical user interfaces to include myriad interaction techniques and devices, multi-modal interactions, tool support for model-based user interface specification, and a host of emerging ubiquitous, handheld and context-aware interactions.”

— John M. Carroll, Author and Founder of the field of Human-computer interaction.


Nowadays, HCI is not merely focused on the interaction with computers, but related to the interaction design, emotional, aesthetics and social engagement. Its principal objective is to develop functional systems that are usable, safe, and efficient for end-users.

HCI is crucial in designing intuitive interfaces that people with different abilities and expertise usually access or who lack in knowledge and formal training on interacting with specific computing systems.
The ultimate goal of HCI is to reduce the amount of effort a user expends when using technology. It aspires to make interactions between people and computers as simple, natural, and open-ended as interactions between people. Therefore, the purpose is designing, implementing, and evaluating interactive interfaces that enhance user experience using computing devices.


From a sign to speech converter...

A wearable hand glove which can convert the sign language (specifically, American Sign Language) to speech output was created by two students at the School of Engineering and Applied Science of Ahmedabad University, in India.
The two students involved in the project were both interested in the functioning of human interaction with the world. In doing research before starting their own project, they came across three major issues:

1. Designing of a glove that could be easily worn and used.
2. Different hand sizes would generate different outputs, as the gestures were different for different hand sizes.
3. The only way in which two speech-impaired people could communicate remotely were video calls. It was therefore impossible for a speech impaired person to take part in an actual phone call.

Before getting their hands dirty on the project, they also spoke to a faculty member in a school for specially abled people located in Bhavnagar, India, coming up with an additional three insights in regards to the students of that school:

1. It was difficult for them to communicate with normal public in public places as majority of people don’t know sign language.
2. They became uncomfortable at the sight of two people having a conversation, wanting to take part in that sort of interaction, but not being physically able to do so.
3. Most of the students coming from rural area didn't have parents literate enough to know the sign language. So for those students, it was also difficult to communicate with people they lived with.

To create the actual glove, one student was responsible for designing the actual glove, while the other student developed the Machine Learning algorithm for generating outputs according to hand gestures. Five flex sensors were used for five fingers which are connected to the micro-controller (Arduino Nano) and a transmitter (Bluetooth Module) is connected to it to transmit the signs to the other systems, like in the picture on the right. A k-NN algorithm was implemented for Machine Learning concepts and supervised learning was used to train the model, and HC05 Bluetooth Module was used to establish communication between two systems.

…Towards successful 3D virtual learning!

A shared online 3D virtual world was created for teaching Human Computer Interaction as a final year course in an undergraduate curriculum. While there is considerable interest throughout academia in using such multi-user virtual environments (MUVE) for education, academics experience many challenges in exploring the various use-cases available when it comes to the use of MUVE.
The purpose of the study was identifying some of the critical issues students might approach when making use of MUVE to learn, as well as possible outcomes of this innovative learning modality.
Using Second Life as their MUVE, 4th year undergrad Computer Science students were assigned the job to build an interactive learning resource which could be used in 1st and 2nd year courses to help learning about a specific algorithm.
Dijkstra's algorithm is used to convert mathematical equations in infix notation to postfix notation, and is known for leaving students quite bored and uninterested. Therefore, the solution developed by the students was supposed to make the learning experience for that algorithm more engaging.

In the end, students were generally very successful in constructing useable and interactive learning resources for the algorithm they were given to work with (an example of a machine proposed by the students is displayed on the image on the left). However, Second Life was described by most participants in the study as unpredictable and unreliable, and project work had to be submitted two or three times due to problems with the ownership and protections model.
In conclusion, while the MUVE concept proved popular and successful, the use of Second Life as a platform seems to have left a lot to be desired.

Where we are now with HCI…

Human-Computer Interaction essentially predates UX (User Experience) Design. The way in which we interact with machines and computers has evolved drastically as humans discover more ways to exploit the technology we have.

UI (User Interface) has in turn increased in importance due the way that mobile devices are paving the way for new and unique interactions between humans and computers, be it for everyday activities to important research across multiple fields of studies.

The amount of computing power our smartphones possess today is truly astounding. For perspective, a mobile device has more computer power than all of NASA had back in 1969, when it sent two astronauts to the moon. These portable devices have enabled various facets for new human-computer interactions, all in the palm of one’s hand.

This video of a baby who, after seeing how to use an iPad (utilising the touch screen to tap and scroll), tries to use the same technique on a paper magazine is a prime example of how HCI is changing the ways of everyday living.

Voice is another type of interaction that is widely spread with programs such as Apple’s Siri, Amazon’s Alexa, and Google Home making it possible to access multiple functions of your smart devices and appliances, allowing the user to search the internet and utilise other simple tasks using merely your voice.

The potential of this type of human-computer interaction is vast, but there are still more ways to fully use the technology we have. Virtual Reality (VR) advancements are also on the rise, currently commonly used for immersive gaming experiences. Smart Wearables have also been in the spotlight now, with how common smart watches are becoming, as these devices become smaller, more featured, and more stylish. These devices are versatile enough to give better user experiences while using commonly used technologies.

HCI in the years to come…

While we are currently witnessing various advancements in the devices we use on a daily basis, there are still improvements to come which will potentially change how we experience technology as users.

Below are some of the most prominent broad areas of technology which are predicted to make huge technological advancements in the coming future.

Voice-Guided User Interfaces

Voice interaction has the potential to go beyond accessing menial tasks and surfing the web. It’s predicted that, in the next few years, the adoption rate of speech recognition will be over 80%. This makes a lot of considering how much smaller and slimmer our screens are getting. With less surface, interfaces will become much harder to navigate. Touch will likely still be the main interface on mobile for a long time to come, however, voice certainly promises a deeper and more ubiquitous method for interacting with our devices.

Virtual and Augmented Reality (VR and AR)

With Meta’s purchase of Oculus and Microsoft entering the VR arena with HoloLens, the competition in the VR landscape is really starting to heat up. These headsets might eventually render TVs obsolete, providing a much more immersive experience when watching videos and playing games. Even today, most people resort to their laptops or computers rather than use TVs to stream shows and movies, and the rise of VR will lessen the use even more.

This new technology will come with a completely new set of human-computer interactions. Once these headsets become commonplace, augmented reality will truly come into its own. These new VR technologies will give rise to so many new and unique interactions, it will truly change the way we design user interfaces and interact with computer systems. Interfaces will no longer be trapped in tiny screens but will instead inhabit our own three-dimensional world.

Wearable Technology

Smart watches are becoming something almost everyone has. Be it a simple one that tracks a person’s vitals and exercise habits to very advanced ones that are practically their own separate devices! The Apple watch for example has already introduced some really unique interactions like the digital crown and the ability to view another person’s pulse. In the coming decade, these devices will become cheaper, more functional and less dependent on your phone, which will in turn make them far more adoptable.

That being said, the wearables market as we know it might not even exist in the next 20-30 years. The current wearables landscape is filled with smart jewellery, smart watches, and even smart shoes. And while these gadgets will have their place in history, they are more-or-less a stopgap. Eventually we will be able to inject this technology directly into our bodies. This means you’ll be able to monitor your most important vital signs without any hardware at all. And just thinking of all the data, you’ll literally have a consistent and accurate record of all of your vital signs throughout your life! The benefits to human health alone could be huge. For now though, the wearables market is slowly gaining momentum and there are a lot of new interactions and design patterns that will come from that.

Conclusions

While we can never accurately predict how far we can go in terms of technological evolution, there’s one thing that remains certain – new forms of HCI will change our lives significantly. They will, undoubtedly, offer the chance to improve the quality of life of people who can’t take advantage of current interfaces due to physical disabilities. As per usual with these drastic changes in technology, new issues will arise such as the ones related to privacy, security, and ethics, thus potentially slowing the diffusion of new hardware and software.

What is amazing about human computer interaction is that just as the mouse and the keyboard were innovations, and the iPhone was a better innovation, the Kinect is also an innovation in response to input technology. But all these are just stepping stones towards less physical interfaces.

The opportunities for HCI are tremendous. Progress toward more user-friendly and natural interfaces for human-machine interaction can yield a plethora of advantages and can majorly impact everyday life.
